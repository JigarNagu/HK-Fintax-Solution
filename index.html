📘 What is Cost Inflation Index (CII)?

The Cost Inflation Index (CII) is notified annually by the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) under Section 48 of the Income Tax Act, 1961. It measures the rate of inflation in India and is used to adjust the purchase price of long-term capital assets to arrive at the "Indexed Cost of Acquisition," thereby reducing the taxable capital gain.

🧮 Formula for Indexed Cost of Acquisition
Indexed Cost = (Original Purchase Price × CII of Year of Sale) ÷ CII of Year of Purchase
📌 Practical Example
Scenario: You purchased a residential property in FY 2010-2011 for ₹25,00,000. You sell it in FY 2025-2026 for ₹90,00,000.

Step 1: CII of purchase year (2010-11) = 632
Step 2: CII of sale year (2025-26) = 1398
Step 3: Indexed Cost = (25,00,000 × 1398) ÷ 632 = ₹55,30,063
Step 4: Taxable LTCG = Sale Price − Indexed Cost = 90,00,000 − 55,30,063 = ₹34,69,937

Without indexation, LTCG would have been ₹65,00,000 — you save significant tax with CII benefit!
✅ When to Use CII (Applicability)
✔ CII Applicable For:
• Long-term capital assets held > 24 months
• Residential property, land, commercial property
• Debt mutual funds (purchased before Apr 2023)
• Gold jewellery, gold ETF (pre-Jul 2024)
• Unlisted shares held > 24 months
• Bonds & debentures (non-equity)
✖ CII NOT Applicable For:
• Short-term capital gains (any asset)
• Listed equity shares / equity mutual funds
• Bonds with indexation specifically excluded
• Depreciable assets (plant, machinery)
• Assets purchased before 01-Apr-2001 (use FMV)
• New tax regime under IT Act 2025
⚠️ Important Notes (Finance Act 2024 Amendment)
From 23 July 2024, the Finance (No. 2) Act 2024 changed LTCG tax on real estate to 12.5% without indexation. However, a grandfathering provision allows properties purchased before 23 July 2024 to choose the lower of: (a) 20% with indexation OR (b) 12.5% without indexation. Indexation benefit continues for other long-term capital assets as applicable.

The Finance Act 2024 has materially changed rules for real estate. 📐 Written Down Value Formula (Section 41)
WDV of Block = [(A − D) + B − C] − E
A = WDV of block in immediately preceding tax year
B = Actual cost of any asset acquired during the tax year, falling within that block
C = Moneys payable (incl. scrap value) for any asset sold/transferred/demolished/destroyed/discarded during the year (capped at A − D + B)
D = Depreciation actually allowed on the block in the preceding tax year
E = Slump sale adjustment, where applicable

🏦 MUTUAL FUNDS — All Types & Concepts
SIP, SWP, STP, Lumpsum, and complete guide to MF categories
🔄 Investment Methods
SIP — Systematic Investment Plan

Regular fixed-amount investment (monthly/quarterly). Rupee cost averaging reduces timing risk. Best for salaried investors building long-term wealth. NAV-based units allotted each instalment. Minimum typically ₹500/month. Can pause/stop anytime. Tax: as per MF category & holding period.

SWP — Systematic Withdrawal Plan

Regular withdrawal from existing MF investment. Used for retirement income or regular cash flow. Corpus remains invested and continues to grow. Only withdrawn amount redeemed. Tax-efficient vs bank FD interest. Each withdrawal treated as redemption — STCG/LTCG applicable.

STP — Systematic Transfer Plan

Automatic transfer from one MF scheme to another (same AMC). Common use: park lump sum in liquid/debt fund → transfer monthly to equity fund. Combines safety of debt with systematic equity averaging. Better than keeping lump sum idle waiting to invest in equity. Each transfer = redemption + subscription (capital gains tax on source fund).

Lumpsum Investment

One-time large investment. Best when markets are at low valuations or during market corrections. Higher timing risk vs SIP. Suitable for investors with large surplus (bonus, windfall). Can combine with STP for risk management. No lock-in except ELSS (3 years). Use when you have high conviction in current market level.

📘 Advance Tax — Quick Reference (Tax Year 2026-27)

Advance tax is mandatory when estimated tax liability after TDS/TCS exceeds ₹10,000 in a year. Effective 1 April 2026, the erstwhile Sections 234B & 234C of the Income-tax Act, 1961 are renumbered as Sections 424 & 425 of the Income-tax Act, 2025 — the rates and mechanism remain unchanged (1% simple interest per month or part thereof).

Section 425 (234C) — Deferment
1% per month, 3 months on shortfall for 1st–3rd instalments, 1 month for the 4th. Relief: no interest for 1st instalment if ≥12% paid; no interest for 2nd if ≥36% paid.
Section 424 (234B) — Shortfall
Applies if advance tax paid by 31 March is less than 90% of assessed tax. 1% per month from 1 April till date of payment of balance tax.
